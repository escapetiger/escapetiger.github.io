Landau equation

February 26, 2026 · 5 min · 946 words
Table of Contents

  • References

The Landau equation models the evolution of the distribution of charged particles in a collisional plasma, particularly in regimes where grazing collisions dominate. Alongside the Boltzmann equation, it is considered one of the most important equations in kinetic theory. Recently, it has garned renewed interest in computational plasma physics due to its significant applications in fusion reactors.

Nonlinear model#

The Landau equation in a $d$-dimensional periodic box is written as

$$ \partial_t f + \mathbf{v}\cdot \nabla_\mathbf{x} f + \mathbf{F}\cdot\nabla_{\mathbf{v}}f = \mathcal{Q}(f,f), $$

where the distribution function $f = f(t,\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})$ depends on time $t\ge 0$, position $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{T}^d$, and velocity $\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ of particles. Field interactions are taken into account by coupling the velocity gradient with an external force $\mathbf{F}$. The binary collision operator $\mathcal{Q}$ is given by

$$ \mathcal{Q}(g,h) = \nabla_\mathbf{v}\cdot\left([\mathbf{A}\ast g]\nabla_{\mathbf{v}}h-[\mathbf{A}\ast \nabla_\mathbf{v}g] h\right), $$

where $\mathbf{A} = (a_{ij})_{d\times d}$ is a positive semidefinite matrix defined by

$$ \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{z}) = |\mathbf{z}|^{\gamma+2}\Pi(\mathbf{z}), \quad \Pi(\mathbf{z}) = \mathbf{I} - \frac{\mathbf{z}\otimes\mathbf{z}}{|\mathbf{z}|^2}. $$

Different values of $\gamma$ lead to the usual classification in hard potentials $\gamma > 0$, Maxwellian molecules $\gamma = 0$, or soft potential $\gamma < 0$. The most interesting case is $\gamma = -3$ where particle are interacted under the Coulomb force. Another case $\gamma = 0$ is associated with a linearization to a linear Fokker-Planck collision preserving the same collisional invariants.

The Landau collision incorporates the Boltzmann collsion with a cutoff Rutherford cross section within the framework of the weak-coupling regime. This groundbreaking work has laid the foundation for understanding the kinetic behavior of particle systems in plasmas and other related fields, emphasizing the importance of collective interactions among charged particles.

Bridging macroscopic world#

Define mascropic density $\rho$, momentum $\mathbf{j}$ and kinetic energy density $E$ as follows:

$$ \rho = \int f(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} ,\quad \mathbf{j} = \rho\mathbf{u} = \int\mathbf{v}f(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} ,\quad E = \frac{1}{2}\rho |\mathbf{u}|^2 + \frac{d}{2}\rho T = \int\frac{|\mathbf{v}|^2}{2}f(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}, $$

where $\mathbf{u}$ denotes the mean velocity of particles, and $T$ is the tempareture given by

$$ T = \frac{1}{d\rho}\int|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}|^2f(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}. $$

The algebraic structure of the Landau operator is similar to the Boltzmann one; this leads to classical collisional invariants $\phi(\mathbf{v}) = (1, \mathbf{v}, |\mathbf{v}|^2)^T$ such that

$$ \int \mathcal{Q}(f,f)(\mathbf{v})\phi(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}. $$

Indeed, the above invariance follows from the weak formulation

$$ \small \begin{aligned} &\int \mathcal{Q}(f,f)(\mathbf{v})\phi(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} \\\\ &= -\frac{1}{2}\int\int\left[\nabla_{\mathbf{v}}\log f(\mathbf{v})-\nabla_{\mathbf{v}'}\log f(\mathbf{v}')\right]^T \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{v}') \left[\nabla_{\mathbf{v}}\phi(\mathbf{v})-\nabla_{\mathbf{v}'}\phi(\mathbf{v}')\right]f(\mathbf{v})f(\mathbf{v}')\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}'. \end{aligned} $$

Testing with collisional invariants briges the kinetic model and the classical macroscopic models, leading to the following balance relations.

  • Local mass conservation
$$ \partial_t \rho + \nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\cdot (\rho\mathbf{u}) = 0. $$
  • Local momentum balance
$$ \partial_t(\rho\mathbf{u})+\nabla_{\mathbf x}\cdot\left(\rho\,\mathbf{u}\otimes\mathbf u+\mathbf{P}\right)=\rho\,\mathbf{F}. $$
  • Local kinetic energy balance
$$ \partial_t E+\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\cdot\left(E\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{P}\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{q}\right) =(\rho\mathbf{u})\cdot\mathbf{F}. $$

In the above equations, $\mathbf{P}$ is the pressure tensor and $\mathbf{q}$ the heat flux defined by

$$ \mathbf{P} = \int (\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u})\otimes(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u})f(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} ,\quad \mathbf{q} = \int\frac{|\mathbf{v}|^2}{2}\mathbf{v}f(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}. $$

With apprpriate physical assumptions on the high-order moments $(\mathbf{P},\mathbf{q})$ and the force $\mathbf{F}$, one could recover the classical hydrodynamic models, such as Euler and Navior-Stokes equations.

Irreversibility and quantitative estimates#

As in Boltzmann case, the formal H-theorem demonstrates the irreversibility of macroscopic evolution despite the time-reversibility of microscopic dynamics for the Landau equation. Specifically, the kinetic entropy is non-increasing in time, i.e.,

$$ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\int\int f\log f\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} \le 0, \tag{H1} $$

and it is conserved in a low-dimensional manifold

$$ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\int\int f\log f\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} = 0 \iff f \in \operatorname{span}\left\{\mathcal{M}\right\}, $$

where $\mathcal{M}$ is the Maxwellian taking the form

$$ \mathcal{M}(\mathbf{v};\mathbf{u},T) = (2\pi T)^{-\frac{d}{2}}\exp\left(-\frac{|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}|^2}{2 T}\right). $$

However, the above qualitivative results neither identify the equilibrium state $f_\infty$ toward which distribution $f$ converges nor provide the quantitative rate of entropy decay.

An extention of Cercignani’s conjecture to the Landau equation reads

$$ \int\int f(t)\log f(t) \mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} - \int\int f_\infty\log f_\infty\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} \le C\exp(-\kappa t), \tag{H2} $$

for some constant $C > 0$ and $\kappa > 0$, where $f_\infty$ is a stationary state solving the equation obtained by setting $\partial_tf = 0$ in the Landau equation. More precisely, it takes the form

$$ f_\infty(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}) = \rho_\infty(\mathbf{x})\mathcal{M}_\infty(\mathbf{v}) ,\quad \mathcal{M}_\infty(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}) = \mathcal{M}(\mathbf{v};\mathbf{u}_\infty(\mathbf{x}),T_\infty(\mathbf{x})), $$

where $\rho_\infty$, $\mathbf u_{\infty}$ and $T_{\infty}$ are the associated macroscopic variables of $f_\infty$.

To prove the conjecture (H2), we start by defining the relative entropy $\mathcal{H}[f|f_\infty]$ as follows:

$$ \mathcal{H}[f|f_\infty] = \int\int f\log\left(\frac{f}{f_\infty}\right) \mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}. $$

Then, we decompose the kinetic entropy as follows:

$$ \int\int f\log f\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} = \mathcal{H}[f|f_\infty] + \int\int f\log f_\infty\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}. $$

We claim that the second term of the right-hand side is equal to the kinetic entropy of stationary state $f_\infty$, i.e.,

$$ \int\int f\log f_\infty\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} = \int\int f_\infty\log f_\infty\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}. $$

Indeed, it suffices to notice that

$$ \int |\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}_\infty|^2f_\infty \mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} = d\rho_\infty T_\infty = \int |\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}_\infty|^2f \mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}. $$

Therefore, the conjecture (H2) is equivalent to

$$ \mathcal{H}[f|f_\infty] \le C\exp(\kappa t). \tag{H3} $$

Next, we compute the time derivative of $\mathcal{H}[f|f_\infty]$ and obtain

$$ \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\mathcal{H}[f|f_\infty] + \mathcal{I}(f) = 0, \tag{E1} $$

where the entropy dissipation $\mathcal{I}$ is given by

$$ \footnotesize \begin{aligned} \mathcal{I}(f) &= -\int \mathcal{Q}(f,f)(\mathbf{v})\log f(\mathbf{v})\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v} \\ &= \frac{1}{2}\int\int \left[\nabla_{\mathbf{v}}\log f(\mathbf{v})-\nabla_{\mathbf{v}'}\log f(\mathbf{v}')\right]^T \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{v}') \left[\nabla_{\mathbf{v}}\log f(\mathbf{v})-\nabla_{\mathbf{v}'}\log f(\mathbf{v}')\right]f(\mathbf{v})f(\mathbf{v}')\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}\mathrm{d}\mathbf{v}'. \end{aligned} $$

The estimate (E1) is referred to as a hypocoercive estimate since

$$ \mathcal{H}[f|f_\infty] \not\lesssim \mathcal{I}(f), $$

In fact, this can be verified by setting $f = \rho\mathcal{M}$ so that $\mathcal{H} > 0$ and $\mathcal{I}(f) = 0$. Moreoever, the kernel of $\mathcal{I}$ is a subspace of the local equilibria manifold, namely,

$$ \mathcal{I}(f) = 0 \iff f = \rho \mathcal{M}. $$

To recover the missing macroscopic dissipation, one could define a modified relative entropy by introducing an additional term. However, unlike the linear Fokker-Planck equation, the dissipation in $\rho$ can not be directly recovered via the equation for momentum $\mathbf{j}$. Therefore, it is a much more challenging but promising direction.
